So I left. Middle of January, no coat, forty-seven dollars in my bank account, and a Civic with a broken heater. I drove away with the windows fogging up and my breath visible in the air, and I didn't look back. That was the last time I saw my mother's face.

I lived in my car for the next six weeks. It was the absolute bottom, the place where the floor drops out and you just keep falling. I parked in Walmart lots and interstate rest stops and, once, in the overflow parking of a twenty-four-hour gym where I'd bought a ten-dollar day pass so I could shower. I learned which library branches let you sit all day without a card. The nights were the worst. Portland in February is a wet, bone-cold kind of misery. I thought about the wedding arch we were supposed to build. The song we were supposed to dance to. The life I was supposed to have. I thought about Mark, and whether he was dating someone new, someone whose family was normal. In those dark hours, I felt a hatred so pure and so hot it scared me. I would lie there and imagine terrible things, things I won't repeat, and then I'd cry until my throat was raw, and then I'd fall asleep from sheer exhaustion. Every morning, the sun would come up, gray and watery through the fogged glass, and I'd wake up alive, and I'd hate that too, for a little while.

I finally got hired at a coffee shop in the Pearl District called Brew Cycle. It paid twelve dollars an hour, plus tips, which on a good day could mean an extra ten or fifteen bucks. It was enough to keep gas in the car and buy food, and it gave me somewhere warm to be during the day. I worked double shifts whenever the manager would let me, picked up extra hours on weekends, did every closing shift that nobody else wanted. Slowly, painfully, over two months, I saved up six hundred dollars. I found a room in a shared house off Foster Road, advertised on Zillow, four women sharing a rundown Craftsman that had been divided into a warren of tiny sleeping rooms. The room was barely bigger than my car backseat. There was a twin mattress on the floor, a bare bulb hanging from the ceiling, and a window that didn't close all the way, letting in a constant draft. But it had a door that locked. I could stretch out flat. I cried from relief the first night. I lay on that mattress, still in my work clothes, and I listened to the house settle around me, and I felt something I hadn't felt in months: a tiny, fragile flicker of safety.

My health fell apart. I lost twenty pounds I could not afford to lose. My hair got thin and brittle. I had chronic headaches, a persistent ache behind my eyes, and my moods swung from numbness to fury to bottomless despair within the same hour. My period stopped for four months from the stress. I existed in a fog of cortisol, just surviving minute to minute. One night, I sat on that mattress and I wrote a list on the back of a receipt. Everyone who had failed me. My parents. Emily. Mark. Even myself, for trusting them, for not seeing the neon signs. I was so angry I felt like my skin was on fire. I wanted to disappear. I stayed up until three in the morning, seriously considering just getting in my car and driving until the gas ran out, somewhere far away where nobody knew my name. I don't know what stopped me. Maybe it was exhaustion. Maybe it was some stubborn little flicker of spite. I fell asleep sitting up against the cold wall, and when I woke up, my back ached and I had to go to work in an hour, and I just kept going.

The turning point came on a Tuesday morning in April. I had been at Brew Cycle for three months by then. I was working the opening shift, which started at five-thirty, and I was moving on autopilot, pulling espresso shots and steaming milk, my body there and my mind a thousand miles away. Around eight, a woman walked in. She was in her late sixties, with short silver hair cut in a sharp bob, bright blue eyes that seemed to take in everything at once, and a navy blazer over straight-leg jeans. She had the kind of casual, confident presence that signals money that doesn't need to announce itself. She ordered a black coffee and a bacon Gruyère scone, and she sat at the corner table with the newspaper. Every morning that week, she came back at the same time, same order. I started to recognize her. She had a way of looking at people, directly and without pretense, that made me feel seen for the first time in years.

One morning, she came up for a refill, and she noticed the book I had tucked under the counter behind the pastry case. It was a beat-up, dog-eared copy of Rich Dad Poor Dad that I'd picked up at a Goodwill for fifty cents. I had been reading during my breaks, partly out of genuine curiosity, partly as a desperate search for some idea that might pull me out of the hole. She nodded at it and asked, "Are you into real estate?" I said I was just curious. I'd always had a vague interest in property. My grandparents had owned a small six-unit apartment building in Southeast Portland when I was a kid, and I used to love going with my grandpa to collect rent and fix leaky faucets. He'd tell me stories about buying it in the seventies for practically nothing, and how it had paid for his retirement. I'd never seriously considered it as a path for me, but the seed was there.

The woman, Linda, didn't let it drop. She started asking me questions. Simple ones at first, like "What do you know about cash flow?" and "Have you ever looked at foreclosure listings?" And before I knew it, over the next several days, the floodgates opened. I told her everything. Not the sanitized version. The whole ugly story. The stolen deposit. The canceled wedding. The lies. The unemployment. The car. The debt. The room with the drafty window. I hadn't told anyone the full story since it happened. I was too ashamed. But Linda listened without a hint of judgment. She let her coffee go cold, and when I finally stopped talking, she said, "You've been through more in two years than most people face in a lifetime. So let me ask you something. Are you going to let it bury you, or are you going to use it as fuel?"

That conversation lit a small pilot light somewhere deep in my chest. Linda, it turned out, was a retired real estate investor. She'd started with nothing in the nineteen-seventies, a single mom working as a secretary, and she'd bought her first duplex with an FHA loan and sheer nerve. Over forty years, she'd built a portfolio of thirty-six units spread across Portland, little by little, deal by deal. She was semi-retired now, managing her properties from a laptop in her condo in the Pearl, but she still had the sharp, hungry mind of someone who loved the game. She started coming to Brew Cycle every single day, and she'd sit at the counter during my breaks and talk. She taught me the basics. Cap rates. Cash-on-cash returns. The seventy percent rule. How to evaluate a neighborhood. How to talk to a motivated seller. She pulled out her phone and showed me her spreadsheets, walking me through the numbers of her best and worst deals. She was blunt and unsentimental. When I said, "I'll never be able to invest because my credit is destroyed and I have no money," she fixed me with a look and said, "Stop making excuses. Money attracts money, but only if you're smart enough to attract it first. You need skills, not cash. Skills can't be repossessed."

That hit me like a freight train. I started devouring every book she recommended. The Millionaire Real Estate Investor. The Book on Rental Property Investing. I listened to podcasts on my shift breaks. I started walking through open houses on my rare days off, just to practice analyzing properties. Linda began taking me with her to look at potential deals on weekends. We'd tour vacant duplexes with peeling paint and tenant damage, and she'd stand in the middle of a living room and mentally calculate rehab costs, pointing at the ceiling, the floors, the windows, rattling off numbers. She taught me how to talk to contractors without getting ripped off. She introduced me to Hector, a patient, big-hearted general contractor with a thick mustache and a booming laugh, who let me follow him around a flip he was working on in Montavilla. I put on work gloves and learned how to patch drywall, how to sand hardwood floors without gouging them, how to install a toilet. My hands ached at night in a way they never had at a keyboard, and it felt like a good kind of pain. Like progress.

Six months after that first conversation, Linda offered me a part-time job as her assistant. Twenty hours a week at eighteen dollars an hour, working from the spare bedroom office in her condo. I'd handle tenant calls, file maintenance requests, coordinate with contractors, organize her paperwork. I kept the coffee shop job on weekends to stay afloat, but I started to breathe again. I paid off the smallest credit card first, then the next one. I moved into a slightly better room in a house with only two other women, a place with actual heat and a window that closed. I bought a space heater at a thrift store for eight bucks, and I slept through the night for the first time in a year.

The next two years were a slow, steady climb. I got my real estate license, studying at the kitchen table until my eyes crossed, and passed the exam on my first try. I joined a small boutique brokerage in Southeast Portland that specialized in first-time homebuyers and fixer-uppers. My first year as an agent, I made twenty-eight thousand dollars in commissions, mostly by working with buyers who had tiny budgets and needed endless hand-holding. It was exhausting and humbling, but I learned how to negotiate, how to read a contract, how to spot a good deal. The second year, I made forty-two thousand. I was still frugal, still driving the Civic with the broken heater, still packing peanut butter sandwiches for lunch, but I was saving.

Linda had become my mentor, my friend, and in many ways the mother figure I'd never truly had. When I'd saved enough, she co-signed on a small fixer-upper for me in the Lents neighborhood because my credit still had dings from the dark years. It was a foreclosure, a three-bedroom ranch with a sagging porch, orange shag carpet that reeked of cat urine, and avocado-green kitchen appliances from the nineteen-seventies. I bought it for one hundred sixty thousand dollars using an FHA 203(k) loan that bundled the purchase and the renovation into one mortgage. I lived in the house during the reno, sleeping on an air mattress in the garage while Hector and his crew tore out the carpet, updated the plumbing, and opened up the kitchen. I painted every wall myself, spent weekends on my knees sanding the hardwood floors, and learned how to do basic electrical work without electrocuting myself. We finished it in four months, and the transformation was stunning. I listed it and it sold in six days for two hundred sixty thousand dollars. After paying off the loan and the commission, I walked away with sixty-two thousand dollars in net profit, tax-free because I'd lived in the property for over two years. I sat on the floor of the empty living room, surrounded by paint cans and the scent of new pine, and I cried. Not sad tears. Relief tears. I was twenty-nine years old. It had taken me three brutal years to claw my way out of the pit. And I had done it.

Over the next two years, I repeated the process like clockwork. I bought a dilapidated Victorian in St. Johns that everyone else overlooked because of structural issues, negotiated the price down to one ninety, put seventy grand of my profit into a gut renovation that Hector managed, and sold it for three hundred ten thousand. I kept one of my flips as a rental, a little bungalow in the Woodlawn neighborhood that cash-flowed three hundred dollars a month. Then I used a cash-out refinance on the rental to buy a duplex, and I fixed it up unit by unit. I formed an LLC to hold my properties, separating my personal assets from my business. I changed my last name from my dad's name to my mom's maiden name, not out of any love for my mom, but because I wanted to erase every single trace of my old life. I became invisible to them. They had no idea where I lived, what I did, or even who I legally was. I paid a lawyer a small retainer to handle any communication if it ever came, but it never did. For four years, I heard nothing from my parents or my sister. Complete radio silence. And I built a life.

By the time I turned thirty-one, I owned four rental units that generated a steady net income of about two thousand dollars a month after all expenses and reserves. I had a healthy six-figure net worth, no consumer debt, and a credit score that had climbed back to seven eighty. I moved into a charming little craftsman in the Woodstock neighborhood with hardwood floors, a big covered front porch with a swing, and a fenced backyard. I rescued a scruffy terrier mix from the Oregon Humane Society and named him Bean. He had one ear that flopped over and a tail that wagged his entire body. I drove a three-year-old Subaru Outback that I'd bought with cash. I had a standing coffee date with Linda every Thursday morning, and she'd become my real family. I was at peace. Genuinely, deeply at peace. I had not spoken to my parents or my sister in over four years. I had made myself into someone they would never recognize. And I was fine with that.

And then, on a random Wednesday in March, the past reached out with a very long arm and tapped me on the shoulder.

My phone rang. The number was unknown, but the area code was local. I ignored it. These days I screened everything. A voicemail came through. I didn't listen to it until the next morning. When I played it, a voice I hadn't heard in half a decade filled my kitchen. Emily. She was crying so hard she could barely form words. In between hiccupping sobs, she said, "Kate, it's Emily. Oh my god, I'm so sorry to call you like this. I know you probably hate me. You have every right to hate me. But I need help. Desperately. Please. I can't explain over voicemail. Please just call me back. I'm begging you. I'm in trouble." She left her number. I stared at my phone. Bean put his head on my knee, sensing my sudden stillness. My heart was hammering. All the old emotions, the ones I'd thought I'd buried under years of paint and spreadsheets and therapy, surged up like a wave. Rage. Grief. Disbelief. And, to my horror, a tiny flicker of hope that maybe, just maybe, she had actually changed.

I called Linda, because I called Linda for everything. She listened to the voicemail on speakerphone, her face impassive. When it was done, she said, "Information before action, kid. Find out what she wants before you decide what to do. You're not the same woman she destroyed. But you need to know the shape of the problem before you walk into the room." So I took a breath, steadied myself, and I called Emily back.

She answered on the first ring. "Kate? Kate, is that really you?" Her voice was high and tight and desperate. She started crying again, and she talked in a messy, tearful flood for almost twenty minutes. The story, when I pieced it together, was this. She and Derek were trying to buy their forever home. A five-bedroom Craftsman in Lake Oswego, of course, with a swimming pool, a three-car garage, and a chef's kitchen with marble countertops. They'd found it after a year of searching, and they'd had an accepted offer of nine hundred fifty thousand. The house was stunning, she said, everything they'd ever wanted. They had a pre-approval from a credit union, but the financing had fallen apart. Derek's credit score had tanked. I wasn't surprised. He'd left his stable job years ago and started a "side business" that turned out to be a series of personal loans propping up a failed app idea. Their debt-to-income ratio was a disaster, and no conventional lender would touch them. They needed a co-signer with strong credit and verifiable income. The only people with that profile were my parents, but my dad had retired and my mom's credit was too leveraged with their own home and a second mortgage they'd taken out after some financial trouble I hadn't known about. So Emily had no one. She said she'd been trying to find me for over a year. She'd hired a private investigator, a woman who'd traced my name change, my LLC, and my new address. She said she was so, so sorry for everything. She'd started going to therapy, she said, and she realized how monstrous she had been. She said she would do anything, absolutely anything, to make it right. She just needed me to co-sign a mortgage so they could buy this house. They would never miss a payment, she swore. I would never be at risk.

I listened to every word without interrupting. I let her hear my silence. When she finally ran out of steam and was just breathing on the line, I said, "I need some time to think about this. Send me the paperwork." She started sobbing again, thanking me, saying God bless you, saying I was the best sister in the world. I hung up and sat at my kitchen table with Bean in my lap. The old rage was there, cold and familiar. The image of her in that hammock with her matcha latte. My mother's arm circling her shoulders. The freezing nights in the Civic, my breath frosting the windows. But there was something else too. A calm, analytical part of my brain, the part Linda had trained, was already working. I pulled up the property records and the loan documents she'd emailed. The house was listed at nine eighty. They had an accepted offer for nine fifty. Their down payment was one hundred forty thousand, cobbled together from savings and a large gift from Derek's parents, leaving a first mortgage of eight hundred ten thousand. To avoid private mortgage insurance and get the best rate, they needed a twenty percent down payment, which meant an extra fifty thousand. They were fifty grand short. The mortgage broker had suggested they find a private second mortgage to bridge the gap. That was where I came in.

I saw the opportunity like a hawk spotting a mouse. I called Emily back the next day. I was calm. I was professional. I said, "Emily, I am not going to co-sign your mortgage. I will never tie my name to your husband's credit or to your joint financial future. But I will do this. I will lend you fifty thousand dollars as a private second mortgage. The note will be secured by a deed of trust on the property, recorded at the county, so I am in a first lien position behind the primary lender. The interest rate will be twenty-five percent annually. You will make interest-only payments for the first sixty months, calculated on the full principal, which comes to a monthly payment of approximately one thousand forty-one dollars. At the end of five years, the full fifty thousand principal is due as a balloon payment. If you make every payment on time, the total interest you'll pay over those five years will be about sixty-two thousand five hundred dollars. However, if you miss even a single payment by more than ten days, the entire balance accelerates, there is a ten percent late penalty on the full amount, plus all legal fees and costs, and I have the right to foreclose immediately."

She was quiet. I could hear her breathing. Then she said, "Twenty-five percent? That's an insane interest rate, Kate. That's predatory." I said, "It's the only offer I'm making, Emily. You stole fifteen thousand dollars from me and tried to destroy my life. You spread a lie that I cheated on my fiancé. You let me sleep in my car while you were snorkeling with sea turtles. If you want my help now, it comes with a price. A very high price. Take it or leave it." She asked for twenty-four hours to think. I knew she would take it. Desperation has a very specific smell, and I could detect it through the phone.

She called back the next morning. Her voice was strained but resolved. "We'll do it. But please, Kate, we won't miss a payment. I promise. I promise on my marriage. We've changed. We really have." I said nothing to that. I called my real estate attorney, a fierce woman named Deborah who had handled my property deals for years. I told her what I wanted. She drafted a promissory note and a deed of trust that were ironclad under Oregon law. Every clause, every penalty, every acceleration trigger was spelled out in plain language, with a separate disclosure page that Emily and Derek would have to initial. I paid Deborah a thousand dollars for the work, and I considered it a down payment on a very satisfying future.

The signing happened at a title company in Tigard. I dressed professionally, a simple black blazer and dark jeans, my hair pulled back. I wanted to look like a woman who would not be moved. Emily had aged in the five years. She was still pretty, but there were fine lines around her mouth and her eyes had a worried, hunted look. She'd lost some of that effortless blonde radiance. Derek was paunchy and sullen, with a weak chin softened further by time. He barely made eye contact with me. The escrow officer, a cheerful man named Greg, walked through each document. I watched Emily's face as she read the interest rate page. Her hand trembled slightly. She initialed it. She signed the promissory note. Derek signed with a scrawl that looked angry. I wired the fifty thousand dollars to escrow that afternoon, and the house closed ten days later. The recorded deed of trust arrived in my mail, a thick envelope I opened with Bean at my feet. I held that paper in my hands and felt a quiet, humming satisfaction. I filed it in my fireproof safe next to my LLC documents and my insurance policies. Then I waited.

The first payment arrived on time, on the first of the month, by wire transfer to my LLC account. One thousand forty-one dollars. The second payment came on the first of the next month, just as reliably. I almost felt a twinge of disappointment. Maybe they were actually going to be responsible. Maybe the threat of losing their dream home was enough to keep them straight. Then the third month came. June fifth was the due date. The grace period extended ten days to June fifteenth. On June tenth, I got a call from Emily. She sounded frantic, her voice high and tight. Derek had been laid off from his job at his father's company six weeks ago, she admitted, and they'd been hiding it. Their emergency fund was gone because they'd had to replace the HVAC system right after moving in, a ten-thousand-dollar hit they hadn't anticipated. They had zero liquidity. She asked, begged, if they could skip one month and make it up over the next two. She promised, swore on everything, that it was just a blip. I kept my voice very even. "Emily, the terms are clear. You signed them. You initialed every page. Payment is due by June fifteenth. If it is not received by end of business on that date, the loan is in default. There is no provision for skipping." She started to cry. "You can't do this to me. I'm your sister." And I said, slowly, savored each word, "You were my sister when you stole my wedding deposit. You were my sister when you told everyone I cheated on Mark. You were my sister when I was sleeping in my car at a Walmart in January, scraping ice off the inside of my windshield. Where was my sister then?" She hung up.

June sixteenth, no payment. I gave it until the end of the day, checking my bank account every hour. Nothing. I followed the procedure exactly as my attorney had outlined. I sent a formal notice of default via certified mail with return receipt. The default triggered the acceleration clause, making the entire balance due immediately: the fifty thousand principal, plus the ten percent late fee of five thousand, plus prepayment of the remaining unearned interest which my attorney calculated and which, with all the penalties, brought the total accelerated balance to just over seventy-eight thousand. I didn't question it. I filed the foreclosure paperwork with the county on June twenty-sixth, exactly ten days after the default. The notice of trustee sale was posted on the property and published in the local paper. The auction was set for September.

Emily called me thirty-seven times in that first week. I didn't answer. She left voicemails that cycled through stages of grief: pleading, sobbing, bargaining, screaming. She called me a monster. She said I was worse than she'd ever been. She said I was going to burn in hell. My parents called too. My mother's voice, older and reedier than I remembered, left a message saying, "How could you do this to your sister? After everything we did for you? We raised you. We fed you. We put a roof over your head. And this is how you repay us? You are dead to me." I didn't flinch. I blocked all their numbers. I blocked their emails. I let the legal machine do its work. I had learned that the system, when properly leveraged, is emotionless and unstoppable.

The auction was held on the county courthouse steps on a crisp September morning. I attended in person, though my attorney could have bid on my behalf. I wanted to see it. There was one other bidder, a man in a polo shirt with a shoulder bag, probably a professional investor. The opening bid was my credit bid of seventy-eight thousand dollars, the amount of my judgment. The man bid eighty. I bid eighty-one. He looked at me, sized me up, and shook his head. He dropped out. I acquired the property subject to the first mortgage, which remained in place and I would need to pay off within the redemption period, but I could sell it before that. The house was now mine. I drove by it that afternoon, a gorgeous Craftsman with a pool in the back, and I felt nothing but a clean, clinical satisfaction.

I listed the house immediately with an agent from my brokerage. The Portland market had heated up over the spring and summer, and I priced it aggressively. The house sold in a bidding war within two weeks for one million twenty-five thousand dollars. After paying off the first mortgage of eight hundred ten thousand, the realtor commissions, the state excise tax, and my legal fees, the net proceeds came to about one hundred eighteen thousand dollars. My original loan had been fifty thousand, and the default penalties and interest legally owed to my LLC totaled sixty-eight thousand. That left a surplus of exactly forty thousand dollars after my own claim was fully satisfied. I had my attorney prepare a detailed settlement statement showing every single line item: the purchase price, the loan payoff, my recovered fees, the surplus, and at the bottom, a line that read, "Deficiency owed by Emily and Derek: forty thousand dollars." But I chose not to pursue that deficiency. Instead, I mailed them the statement. No check. No surplus return. Just the bill, itemized down to the penny, with a single handwritten line at the bottom in my own careful script: "Now we're even." I sent it certified mail, return receipt, so I would know the exact moment they opened it.

I never heard from Emily again. The receipt came back signed by Derek. Two months later, a mutual acquaintance from the old days reached out to me, maybe fishing for gossip, and told me what had happened. Emily and Derek had filed for bankruptcy. They'd lost the short sale opportunity because I'd already foreclosed, so the home was gone. They rented a cramped two-bedroom apartment in Gresham, near a busy highway. Derek filed for divorce a year later, and their marriage dissolved like sugar in hot water. Emily got a job as a receptionist at a dental office in Clackamas, a forty-minute commute from their rental, making maybe eighteen bucks an hour. My parents, to try to help them cover legal fees from the bankruptcy, sold their beloved Lake Oswego house, the house I'd grown up in, and downsized to a condo in Salem. The equity they'd spent decades building was gone in a matter of months. I heard that my dad had a stroke not long after, not fatal but debilitating, and he spent his days in a recliner watching Fox News. My mother, the woman who had told me to move on, was now a full-time caregiver. I didn't feel glee. I didn't feel guilt. I felt the quiet, steady click of a door closing for good.

As for me, I took that forty thousand dollar profit and I rolled it into a down payment on a six-unit apartment building in Milwaukie, a value-add property that needed a lot of work. Hector and I are still renovating it, unit by unit, and when it's fully stabilized, it will cash flow beautifully. Bean still greets me at the door of our little house in Woodstock, his whole body wagging. Linda passed away last fall, at seventy-eight, peacefully in her sleep at home. I cried for days. I wore her favorite navy blazer to her memorial service and I spoke about the barista she saw in a coffee shop and decided to invest in. She left me her entire portfolio, minus a few properties she donated to a women's shelter. I manage them now, thirty-six units that she spent a lifetime building, entrusted to me. Every time I walk through one of those buildings, I think of her. I still go to Brew Cycle sometimes, to the corner table, and I order a black coffee and a scone. I read the paper. I know the new baristas by name. And when someone asks me about my life, I just smile and say, "I've been through a few things."

Edit: A lot of you are asking about the legality of that interest rate. In Oregon, private loans between individuals are not subject to the same usury caps as institutional consumer loans, provided both parties are sophisticated and have independent representation. I made sure Emily and Derek each had the opportunity to consult their own attorney, which they waived in writing. Twenty-five percent is high, but the promissory note was drafted by an experienced real estate lawyer and signed by all parties with full disclosure. I never do anything that could be overturned in court. The paperwork is airtight.

Edit 2: To the people saying I should have just said no and walked away, I understand the impulse. But you don't know what it's like to be systematically discarded by the people who are supposed to love you. She came to me. She begged. I gave her exactly what she asked for, with clear, documented terms she freely accepted. She had every chance to find another lender, sell some assets, lower her ambitions. She chose the fastest, riskiest path. I didn't ruin her. She ruined herself with a house she couldn't afford and a marriage built on sand. I was just the one holding the note when it all fell apart. I gave her the same consideration she gave me: none.

Edit 3: Yes, I'm aware that in a normal foreclosure, any surplus after the debt and costs are satisfied typically goes back to the borrower. But my promissory note included a specific clause that any surplus from a sale would be applied first to an additional liquidated damages provision equal to the total remaining unearned interest over the full five-year term, plus default penalties. That total exceeded the surplus, so the forty thousand was retained by the lender. The disclosure sheet explained this clearly. Her attorney explained it. She initialed that page in front of a notary. I made absolutely sure of it. She had no one to blame but herself.

Edit 4: I still think about that moment when I handed her the opportunity on a silver platter, laced with enough risk to be exactly what she deserved. Part of me, a tiny part, wonders what would have happened if I'd just forgiven her. Maybe I'd be the bigger person. But being the bigger person got me nothing but frostbite and fourteen grand in credit card debt. The day I mailed that itemized bill, I came home, hugged my dog, and felt, for the first time in five years, like my lungs could fully expand. So tell me: what would you have done? Because I'd do it all again, exactly the same, and the only thing I regret is that she didn't read that bill while I was watching. That would have been delicious.
