It was a throwaway moment, a small leather journal with a broken brass clasp that my sister Renee tossed into a black contractor bag without a second thought. I dug it out of the trash that same night, and on the last page, in my mother’s shaky cursive, were seven words that would eventually dismantle an entire scheme, trigger a federal Medicaid fraud investigation, and send my sister to prison. This is the story of how I turned a fake identity, a job in a nursing home, and three years of silent paper-trail gathering into a six-figure restitution judgment that left Renee disgraced and permanently banned from ever seeing our mother again.

My name is Claire, and I was a bookkeeper for a small manufacturing firm, a woman who had spent two decades reconciling ledgers and tracking decimal-place errors that made other people’s eyes glaze over. I was the daughter who moved back into the spare bedroom of my mother’s condo on Elm Street, a modest two-bedroom unit she’d bought with thirty years of teaching algebra to high school freshmen. The condo smelled like chalk dust and lavender hand soap, and I knew every creak in the hallway floor because I’d walked it four times a night when her sundowning episodes started. My sister Renee was a marketing director in Scottsdale, married to a commercial real estate agent named Darren, and their life was a curated scroll of white wine on a deck and granite countertops. Mom, the quiet architect of our family’s financial stability, had always trusted me to handle the checkbook and the calendar, but Alzheimer’s doesn’t care about loyalty, and by the time she couldn’t recognize my face, Renee was already rewriting the story.

The slide into betrayal happened thirty-six days after a meeting I replay every night. Renee flew in unannounced with a leather portfolio and a durable power-of-attorney document she’d already drafted with a lawyer I’d never met. She gathered our aunt and uncle in Mom’s living room, dabbed at eyes that stayed dry, and painted me as a woman shattered by grief, too emotional to manage a utility bill. She used words like “caregiver burnout” and “cognitive fog,” and she referenced a single missed credit card payment, a forty-dollar minimum I’d overlooked the week Mom stopped recognizing the bathroom, as proof I was unraveling. I sat on the floral sofa holding Mom’s hand while she stared at a crack in the ceiling, and within ninety minutes Renee had convinced my relatives that I needed to be removed from all financial and healthcare decisions. They signed statements supporting her as sole durable power of attorney, and she left the next morning with notarized forms, the key to Mom’s safety deposit box, and a plan I didn’t see coming.

The major loss arrived with the speed of a slammed door. Six days before the medical transport van showed up, Renee walked into the county recorder’s office with a quitclaim deed that transferred sole ownership of the two-hundred-and-eighty-thousand-dollar condo to herself for zero dollars in consideration. The document bore a signature that looked like mine, a practiced slant she’d done on scratch paper until it fooled a notary. Then she called a long-term care facility in rural Ohio, nearly six hundred miles from my home, and arranged a bed in the Medicaid wing, the kind with a shared room and a roommate who screamed at shadows. A white van with tinted windows arrived at eight a.m., and two men in polo shirts loaded my mother inside with a duffel bag of mismatched clothes, no family photo, no comfort object, no one to tell her where she was going. I stood in the driveway and watched the van disappear, and I didn’t know then that the condo was already gone, that my mother’s only asset had been stripped before she was even loaded onto the gurney.

The day after the transport, the vultures circled in earnest. Renee and Darren showed up with a rented dumpster and a crew of two men from a liquidation company who moved through the rooms like they were cataloging inventory. Darren photographed furniture to list online while Renee pulled jewelry boxes, silverware, and my mother’s address books into plastic bins. I stood in the hallway, hollow and disoriented, watching my childhood home be dismantled. Renee held up a stack of worn journals and recipe cards and called them trash. She scooped them into a black contractor bag, including one small leather-bound volume with a brass clasp that had snapped years ago. She dropped it without a second look, muttering something about “old lady scribbles.” I waited until noon when they broke for lunch, walked over to the dumpster, and fished the journal from the top of the bag. I sat on the curb and flipped through pages of grocery lists, half-finished crosswords, and one entry where Mom had written, “Renee asked about the deed again. I told her Claire handles that.” On the very last page, in tight, deliberate cursive, she had pressed the pen so hard it dented the paper. The words were: “Do not trust them. Save yourself.” Beneath that, a single initial, M, and a date from three years earlier, right around the time Renee had first started probing about Mom’s estate plan. I slipped the journal under my shirt and walked back inside, my insides cold with a clarity that hadn’t been there before.

Within forty-eight hours, Renee shifted from liquidator to someone who wanted to appear like a caregiver. She sat me at Mom’s kitchen table with a cup of chamomile tea and a stack of documents she said would “consolidate things” and “protect Mom’s legacy.” The top sheet was a new power-of-attorney form, this one authorizing her to manage my personal assets as well, since I was clearly too fragile to handle my own checking account. She’d also brought a blank authorization to close my savings account and transfer the balance to an escrow she would “oversee” for my benefit. Darren hovered behind her, nodding sympathetically and mentioning the guest room in their five-bedroom house back in Scottsdale, with a mountain view and a pool. He said things like “we’ll take care of everything” and “you need to focus on healing, not bills.” I could feel the notary stamp in their bag before I even saw it, ready to execute the papers the moment I stopped resisting. I kept my voice soft, my movements slow, my eyes unfocused. I told them I couldn’t think straight, that everything felt like fog, and I pushed the papers aside without signing a single line. They didn’t notice that my hands weren’t trembling. They saw what they wanted to see.

I was in the back seat of their SUV forty-eight hours later, watching the desert roll past while Renee talked about fresh starts and healing. Her real goal was control. With me under her roof, she could monitor my phone calls, intercept my mail, and eventually wear me down until I signed whatever release she needed to make the condo theft legally bulletproof. I played the part of the broken little sister, the one who cried quietly at dinner and needed help finding the bathroom. But every single night, after the house settled and the central air clicked off, I would sit on the edge of the guest room bed, window locked, camera doorbell blinking outside, and I would read those seven words in Mom’s journal. My fog wasn’t real, and it was hardening into a plan.

The undercover investigation began in that house. I paid attention to the rhythms of their lives, and the first thing I noticed was the money. Darren handled the finances, and he handled them badly. Every morning at seven-fifteen, I’d hear him in the home office, voice pitched low and desperate on the phone with someone he called a “servicing agent” or a “bridge lender.” He’d say things like, “We just need another thirty days” or “The credit line can’t freeze now.” Then he’d hang up, curse, and pace the tile floor so hard I could map his route from the creaks. One morning I heard him say the words “balloon payment” and “we’re upside down” and then the clatter of a phone being thrown onto the desk. The walls in that house were thin enough that I could hear the panic spike in his voice every time a call went unreturned. Renee spent money like the spigot would never dry. A brand-new Range Rover appeared in the driveway two days after I arrived, the sticker still from the dealer. A backyard pool renovation started within a week, complete with a waterfall and a custom tile mosaic. Subscription boxes piled on the porch every other day, and I saw a credit card statement left on the kitchen island once, with a balance of forty-seven thousand dollars and a minimum payment they were barely making. Their entire lifestyle was a balloon inflated on borrowed money and fraud, and I could sense the popping point if I just kept watching.

The first real access I got came from a household accident that exposed who actually knew how to fix things. The garbage disposal jammed on a Sunday morning, a wet grinding noise that filled the kitchen, and Darren stood over the sink with a butter knife, poking at the blades and making it worse. Renee yelled about calling an emergency plumber on a weekend, and when they retreated to the patio in frustration, I walked to the sink. I reached under the cabinet, pressed the thermal reset button on the disposal’s base, and then took a hex wrench from the tool drawer I’d scouted earlier. I inserted the wrench into the socket on the bottom and turned the impeller counterclockwise until the jam released with a soft click. The whole fix took ninety seconds, and when they came back in, the water drained smoothly. I shrugged and said I must have gotten lucky, and they looked at me like I’d performed a magic trick. In that moment, their helplessness was confirmed, and my confidence solidified. I was not the fragile one. I never had been.

That small success gave me the nerve to map the home office and its locked contents. The room had a heavy oak desk with a locked file drawer, a shredder basket overflowing with strips of paper, and a metal filing cabinet in the corner with a key lock I recognized immediately. I’d spent two summers in college working for a locksmith named Gus, learning to rekey deadbolts, install panic bars, and pick simple wafer locks. The filing cabinet’s lock was a four-wafer mechanism, and one night when Renee and Darren attended a charity gala, I retrieved the small leather pick case I’d hidden in the lining of my suitcase. I used a tension wrench inserted into the bottom of the keyway and a short hook pick to rake the wafers, feeling each one set with a tiny metallic click until the plug turned. Inside, organized by colored tabs, was everything. The original quitclaim deed with my forged signature, the notary stamp with a date six days before Mom’s transport, a Medicaid application for Mom dated ten days after the condo transfer, and a stack of bank statements showing accounts Renee had never disclosed. The application listed Mom as having zero real property and less than two thousand dollars in liquid assets, when in truth the condo was worth two hundred eighty thousand and had been in her name until a pen stroke erased it. I also found a folder labeled “Elder Law” with a letter from a Phoenix attorney advising Renee on the Medicaid look-back period and the importance of “divesting assets without obvious paper trail.” She’d highlighted the line about the five-year penalty window and stapled a sticky note that said, “Mom’s condo must be out of her name before she applies.”

I spent nearly two hours that night photographing every page with my phone. I laid the documents on the floor, angled a desk lamp for perfect light, and captured the account numbers, the routing codes, the date stamps, and the telltale slant of my forged signature. Then I turned to the desk drawer. The lock was a simple pin-tumbler, cheap enough that I raked it open in under thirty seconds. Inside was Darren’s spiral notebook with handwritten notes in blue ink. One page read, “Claire signature practice,” and listed five iterations of my name with varying loops. Another page had a sticky note with a username and password for a cloud storage folder labeled “MOM ASSETS,” and another page listed the login for a secondary email account tied to a brokerage firm where I’d later find records of the liquidated investment portfolio. I didn’t take anything physical that night. I closed the drawers, wiped my prints, and left with sixty-three photographs and a cold fury that kept me awake until dawn. I also noted a shredded document in the wastebasket that I quietly reassembled the next day while they were out: a notice from their mortgage lender threatening acceleration for non-payment, a clear sign that the condo theft was a desperate liquidity grab, not just greed.

The ally in this story arrived through a letter I almost never received. Three weeks later, while sorting through a pile of forwarded mail that a neighbor had kindly collected from Mom’s old address, I found a cream envelope from Robert Choi, an estate attorney in our hometown. Inside was a handwritten note explaining that my mother had visited him eighteen months before her diagnosis worsened, right around the time Renee started asking about “future planning.” Mom had left a notarized affidavit and a video recording in a safety deposit box at First Mutual Bank, to be released only if a transfer of real property occurred without the written consent of both daughters. The box required both my physical key and Renee’s signature to open, a dead-man’s-switch that Renee didn’t know about fully because Mom had never trusted her with the details. Robert’s letter also mentioned that the box contained a copy of a title restriction on the condo, filed with the county recorder, that would flash an alert if ever a quitclaim deed was recorded without both daughters present. He wrote, “Your mother anticipated this. She said you would know what to do.” I called Robert Choi from a burner phone I’d bought at a gas station, and his voice was calm and steady. He said, “Your mother was a lot smarter than anyone gave her credit for. Come in, and I’ll show you the recording.”

I watched that video in a small conference room with the blinds drawn, a box of tissues I never used. My mother sat in a chair facing the camera, her hair still dark, her eyes clear, and she spoke directly to me. She said, “Claire, if you’re watching this, it means I knew what your sister would try to do. The condo is yours in spirit, but legally I’ve set up a trap. Renee can’t sell it without your signature, and if she tries to take it without you, the bank will freeze everything and alert my attorney. She’ll need you to open this box, and she’ll come to you eventually. When she does, don’t be alone. I’ve also filed a notice with Medicaid’s integrity program, so if Renee tries to enroll me under false pretenses, it will flag on a future audit. You just have to stay alive, stay quiet, and find the paper trail. I love you.” Then the screen went dark. I sat in silence for a full minute, and then Robert slid a business card across the table. On it was the name and number of Investigator Denise Warren, with the Arizona Attorney General’s Medicaid Fraud Control Unit. He told me she was already looking into a pattern of similar cases, and my evidence could build the bridge.

I met Denise three days later at a coffee shop twelve miles from Renee’s house. I wore a ball cap, no makeup, and I handed her a thumb drive with all sixty-three photographs. She plugged it into her laptop right there, and I watched her face cycle from professional calm to something colder as she scrolled through the forged deed, the Medicaid application, and the brokerage statement I’d found in the filing cabinet showing a thirty-four-thousand-dollar investment account Renee had closed out and funneled into a joint account with Darren. Denise closed the laptop and said, “This is interstate elder exploitation, forgery, perjury, and a five-year Medicaid look-back violation. But to make it stick, I need internal facility records showing the billing fraud from the nursing home side. The look-back period requires us to prove she knew Mom’s assets existed when she declared her assetless for Medicaid. If you can get inside that facility and copy their cost reports, timeline of services, and Medicaid remittance data, we can prove Renee knew she was committing fraud every single month.” She couldn’t ask me to do it, she said, but she gave me the name of the corporate staffing agency that contracted personal companions to Maple Grove Care Center, the very facility in rural Ohio where Mom was warehoused. And she slid a small device across the table, a USB hardware keylogger, no bigger than a thumb drive. She said, “If you ever get access to their billing computer, this plugs in between the keyboard and the tower. It captures every keystroke, including passwords. Use it, then mail it back to me.”

I became Sarah Holloway four months later. The transformation was complete and methodical. I cut my hair into a short gray bob, let my natural brown roots go untouched for weeks, bought thick-framed glasses with plain lenses, and practiced a slightly lower, softer voice until it felt like a second skin. A friend from my bookkeeping days, a woman named Jo who had been erased by her own family once, helped me obtain a realistic fake driver’s license and a clean background check for a price that made my stomach turn, but I paid it. I applied to Caring Hands Staffing as a personal companion, listing three years of fabricated experience in private home health, backed by references who were actually Jo and two of her cousins, all prepared to lie. The facility hired me at fourteen dollars an hour, and on my first day I walked the linoleum hallways wearing scrubs the color of a faded robin’s egg, a name tag that read Sarah, and a backpack containing a digital voice recorder, a small notepad, and a set of charging cables. My mother was in room one-oh-seven, and she didn’t know me.

The heart of this story is those three years underground. I showed up every morning at seven a.m., signed in at the logbook, and began my rounds. My official job was to provide companionship to a handful of memory-care residents, guiding them to the activity room, reading aloud from old magazines, and sitting with them during meals. I requested my mother’s hall specifically, a quiet wing with a lingering smell of institutional soup and floor wax, and I made sure to learn the rhythms of the entire facility. The business office was a cramped, windowless room with a dinosaur of a PC that hummed constantly, a dot-matrix printer that printed Medicaid remittance forms on perforated paper, and a filing system that overflowed into hallway carts every weekend. The billing clerk was a tired, kind woman named Dee who wore cardigans with missing buttons and drank coffee from a thermos that had seen better decades.

I made myself indispensable slowly. I started by helping residents with meals, learning their preferences, and then I mentioned my “background in numbers” one afternoon when Dee was complaining about an error in the quarterly cost reports. She let me cross-check a spreadsheet, and I corrected three thousand dollars in misallocated pharmacy charges within an hour, tracing the codes back to the wrong general ledger account. After that, I became her unofficial audit partner. Within four months, I had read-only access to the facility’s billing software, and I began building a parallel paper trail. I printed care-level assessments, monthly invoices, and the state’s remittance advices that showed exactly how much Medicaid paid for my mother based on the false asset declaration Renee had submitted. I also found the internal “resident financial verification” form where Renee had checked a box affirming Mom had no real property, no investments, and no cash value life insurance. I copied every screen, every coded entry, and every discrepancy, and I organized them into a binder that I hid in the ceiling panel above the staff bathroom. Every week, I’d add another ten pages, cross-referencing dates with the bank statements I’d already photographed, building an unassailable timeline: the condo deed was recorded on March 4th, the Medicaid application was signed on March 14th, and the first Medicaid payment to the facility hit on April 1st, a sequence that proved Renee knew she was gaming the system.

Then came the keylogger. One Friday afternoon, when Dee left early and the business office was empty, I slipped inside. The back of the computer tower was dusty and warm. I disconnected the keyboard cable, plugged the USB keylogger into the port, and reconnected the keyboard to the logger, just as Denise had shown me. It was invisible unless someone crawled under the desk. I left, heart pounding, and returned a week later to retrieve it. That tiny device captured everything: Dee’s login password, the Medicaid provider portal credentials, and even internal emails where the facility administrator noted Renee’s frantic calls about the state audit, asking to be notified before any records were released. One email read, “Spoke with daughter Renee again. She’s very concerned about the audit. Wants to know what documents we’re releasing. Remind her we must comply with state law.” I burned the captured data onto a CD and mailed it to Denise in a padded envelope with no return address, wearing latex gloves the whole time.

During those three years, I uncovered more than I had hoped. Renee had not stopped at the condo. She’d liquidated the thirty-four-thousand-dollar investment account I’d already found evidence of, but I discovered she’d also cashed out a small life insurance policy Mom held, worth twelve thousand dollars, by forging Mom’s signature on a surrender form. That document was in a forgotten filing box in the facility’s administrative closet, buried under old employee timesheets and holiday schedules. I found it on a Tuesday morning while “organizing” old files, and I photographed every page. The paper trail now showed a total of over one hundred twenty thousand dollars in stolen assets and improper Medicaid benefits. Later, I accessed the cloud storage folder using the credentials from Darren’s notebook, from a library computer three towns over, and found scanned copies of the forged surrender form, as well as a spreadsheet tracking the “cash flow” from Mom’s accounts into their joint checking account, with notes like “use for pool contractor.” Denise updated me through coded messages left in a free email account we both accessed, never sending a direct message, always saving drafts. The net was tightening.

Renee visited the facility exactly twice during those three years. The first time, she spent twenty minutes in the business office, not the patient room, asking about the chain of command for “financial approvals.” I stood in the hallway, pretending to wipe down a handrail, and recorded her voice through the gap in the door frame. The second visit came during year two, when the state sent a notice of a random audit and she panicked. I saw her stride in wearing a tailored blazer and a tight smile that didn’t reach her eyes. She never even glanced at Mom. She went straight to Dee’s office and demanded copies of billing records, gesturing at spreadsheets she couldn’t read, her voice high and reedy. I recorded that entire conversation too, and in my recordings you can hear Dee’s confused, polite responses and Renee’s sharp, demanding tone. That audio became a central piece of evidence for undue influence and consciousness of guilt.

The maximum cruelty beat arrived ten months before the final confrontation. By then, I knew the criminal case had enough for an indictment, but I needed to know if any scrap of human feeling remained in my sister’s chest. So I engineered a staged medical scare. I planned it for a week when I’d overheard Renee was flying in to meet with a real estate agent about listing the condo. I sent a text from a burner phone to her primary number, posing as a nurse, stating that Margaret H. had taken a sudden turn and the facility needed an immediate medical directive because a decision about intubation was required, and they couldn’t reach me. Renee showed up in forty minutes, not because she cared, but because she was terrified someone else might gain access to financial documents if Mom died without her there.

I waited until she rounded the corner near the activity room, then I let my body drop. I went limp against the wall, sliding down, my eyelids fluttering, my hand clutching at my chest, and I did it with enough realism that two CNAs rushed toward me. Renee walked directly past my crumpled form. She stepped over my outstretched ankle without even slowing, and through my slitted eyes, I saw her yank open the drawer of Mom’s bedside table, rifling through insurance cards and old receipts, searching for a document I’d purposely mentioned in the fake text. She did not help. She did not pause. I was nothing but an obstacle, a body on the floor. That linoleum was cold against my cheek, and the last flicker of familial loyalty I carried died right there, as the CNA’s voice called for a crash cart and my sister’s heels clicked away. After I got up, one of the CNAs, a woman I’d quietly befriended over the years, whispered, “She didn’t even look at you.” I nodded and said, “I know.”

The confrontation came six months after that. The Attorney General’s office had secured sealed indictments for elder exploitation, Medicaid fraud, and forgery, and the arrest was planned for a Tuesday morning at the First Mutual Bank branch where Renee had scheduled a signing. She believed she was finally going to force me to consent to a clean title transfer, because her developer deal was closing and the buyer needed clear ownership. By then, I had revealed my true identity to the facility administration with a court order protecting my mother’s wellbeing, and I was working openly with Denise. She called me the night before and said, “She doesn’t know we have the forgery. She thinks you’re still helpless and isolated. When she tries to get your signature, don’t sign, don’t explain. Just watch her face when the door opens.”

At nine forty-five a.m., I walked into the bank lobby dressed as myself, the sister Renee hadn’t seen in over four years. I wore a simple blouse and slacks, and I carried no bag, no weapon, just the seven words from that journal burning in my chest. Renee was already there with Darren, a notary in a stiff black suit, a real estate agent tapping on a tablet, and a stack of closing documents an inch thick. She had tracked me down through a skip tracer and had been leaving voicemails for weeks, acting magnanimous, saying she’d “forgive” my past if I just helped settle Mom’s affairs. The performance almost made me laugh. She stood when she saw me, arms slightly open, and began a rehearsed speech about closure and family and how we could all move forward. I sat down at the conference table, folded my hands on the cool wood, and let her monologue wind down until she pushed the signature page toward me.

I did not touch the pen. I looked directly into her eyes, into the irises that shared my genetic code, and I said, “You forged my signature once. You stepped over my body when you thought I was dying. But I’ll never give you a second one.” The confusion on her face shifted through disbelief, then panic, as the bank’s conference room door opened. Three people walked in: Investigator Denise Warren with her badge displayed on a lanyard, a county sheriff’s deputy with a hand resting on his belt, and Robert Choi, the estate attorney, holding a certified copy of the forged deed. Darren started to say something about lawyers, but the deputy was already reciting Miranda rights, and the real estate agent scrambled to gather her tablet and flee. The notary went pale. Renee collapsed into a chair, her marketing polish gone, replaced by a raw, ugly terror that I had never seen before. The authorities seized every document on the table, and the bank’s compliance officer, who had been briefed that morning, confirmed that a lien had been placed on the condo, freezing all transactions. Her sale was dead. Her fraud was exposed. And the woman she had spent years painting as incompetent had just pulled the entire floor out from under her.

The aftermath took fourteen months of legal proceedings, and I attended every hearing. Renee ended up pleading guilty to two felonies, forgery and fraudulent schemes, and she accepted a sentence of four years in prison with five years of supervised probation. Darren, who had signed some of the forged documents as a witness, was convicted of conspiracy and sentenced to two years, though his was stayed pending appeal. The restitution order totaled one hundred fifty-three thousand dollars, covering the stolen investment account, the improper Medicaid benefits, and the civil penalties for elder exploitation. The court voided the quitclaim deed, ruling it a product of forgery and undue influence, and the condo reverted to Mom’s estate. With the trust terms requiring both daughters’ consent for any sale, and Renee now a convicted felon, the probate judge appointed me as sole conservator. I moved Mom from Maple Grove to Sunridge, a top-tier memory care unit with a secure garden, live piano music on Wednesdays, and a window overlooking a pond with actual ducks. I funded the entire monthly cost with the seized assets from Renee’s restitution. The state auctioned Renee’s Scottsdale home, her Range Rover, and even that pool with the waterfall, and the proceeds were liquidated into a care fund. Every single month, when the statement arrives and I see the payment clearing, I take a quiet moment to remember that it’s Renee’s own greed that keeps our mother safe.

The final piece of justice came eight months after the sentencing, when Renee, temporarily released for a supervised medical furlough, attempted to enter Sunridge wearing a volunteer’s polo shirt and a short black wig. She told the front desk she wanted to “surprise her mother,” but her photo was already in the facility’s banned-persons binder, printed and laminated. A staff member on the security desk recognized her immediately and blocked the entrance, and I was called from the garden terrace. I walked to the lobby and found her there, red-faced and shaking, clutching a cheap bouquet of grocery-store carnations. I crossed my arms and told her, calmly, that if she ever set foot on this property again, I would press charges for violating the permanent protective order and she would be back in a cell before sunset. She shrieked, called me a monster, and then two campus security officers escorted her out the main doors while she kept shouting that I’d stolen everything from her. The ban is now legally permanent, recorded in two county systems, and on Mom’s best days, when she surfaces from the fog for a few blessed minutes and smiles at me, I know she understands something bigger than her disease.

What I’ve learned is that my mother was never just a math teacher. She was a quiet genius who saw the cracks in my sister’s character decades before the rest of us, and she left me a ledger I just had to learn how to read. Her note in that little leather journal was not a desperate plea. It was a set of instructions, and every step I took was me finally decoding it. Integrity doesn’t announce itself. It hides in a broken clasp, in a locked filing cabinet, and in the hundreds of small, brutal choices to keep showing up when no one is watching. That is the inheritance no court can seize, and it’s the one I live inside every day.
